3d PrintingProsthetic Sockets

Undergraduate Research

I worked in a group with three other undergraduate students and a research advisor during my first year of college on a research project with the goal of assessing virtual modeling methods and the mechanical properties of 3D printed transtibial prosthetic sockets. We were able to evaluate and compare the conventional method of producing sockets with respect to the 3D printed method. The conventional method of required manually making a negative and subsequently positive plaster mold from the residual limb. Technicians then pull a heated sheet (often thermo-plastic) over the positive mold to produce a socket. Some difficulties with this method is making the thickness of the socket uniform, and making a perfect fit in a reasonable amount of time. No leg is the same between people, so each socket must be customly made to fit. Often, revisions are necessary if the socket does not fit properly. If it does not fit properly, it will require revisions to eliminate any discomfort, pain, or residual limb skin breakdown.

3D printing methods has been explored to make the sockets more exact and make them is less time. This approach may reduce human errors as well as the manufacturing time. The goal of this project was to explore and report 3D modeling methods to transform actual patient scans into test socket models which may be 3D printed then evaluate the mechanical characteristics of those sockets. Our methods consisted of developing the 3D modeling routines to transform a positive model of the residual limb into that of a socket and develop mechanical testing parameters to evaluate the socket. The end product was a solid model that can be sent to the 3D printer for fabrication. The STL files needed to be converted into a surface mesh before being converted into a solid body. We finally wanted to test and compare the strength of the 3D printed sockets by producing an axial load using the Tinius Olsen H10KS machine.
